Lesli Rambin


A native of West Monroe, Louisiana, Lesli Rambin is employed as an English Instructor by the University of Louisiana at Monroe, where she earned her Master’s in Teaching in 2011 and her Bachelor’s in English in 2006. She also earned her Master’s in Creative Writing from the University of Louisiana at Lafayette in 2008.
In addition to teaching, Lesli serves as a contributing music editor for the online literary journal, The Swamp, and cohosts a weekly local public radio segment, “Byway Blues,” with her husband, Kirby. Lesli and her husband are more than spouses; they are research partners, co-workers, best friends and soul mates. When not working, they are most likely together, listening to one of their beloved records, sharing an Abita Amber and the company of their dog.
Her love for Louisiana can be seen not simply in her dedication to establishing a Louisiana Blues Trail, but in her preference for strawberries over any kind of meat, her passionate pursuit of magnolias in May, her tendency to seek out obscure, country roads to travel down, and her embrace of all things that evoke that sweet southern gothic beauty. Above all else, she strives to preserve and promote regional culture, believing that we should never fail to see the beauty in our humble origins.



Kirby Rambin


Kirby Rambin, born in Monroe, teaches Orchestra at the Excellence Academy Charter Middle School. A student of the violin for over 23 years, Kirby has performed with artists such as Doug Duffey, Larry Garner, Toussaint McCall, and Kenny Bill Stinson.
Kirby and his wife, Lesli are both contributing music editors for “The Swamp” – an online literary journal. They also cohost the weekly “Byway Blues” segment on the local public radio station, KEDM, where Kirby is also employed as a Production Assistant. Together, they work to collect the music and stories of Louisiana’s rich and cultured past. This love of their home state’s culture is second only to their love of each other.
Louisiana flows through Kirby’s veins, as his ancestors were some of the first European settlers in the area. His work on the Louisiana Blues Trail comes naturally out of his love for the land, people, and culture of his home. He hopes to help more people see the splendor in Louisiana’s colorful and complicated past.


Mission Statement


MISSION: The Louisiana Blues Trail seeks to preserve Louisiana’s unique blues history through recognizing small towns as cultural epicenters and using regional folk art to commemorate the musicians and land, thereby fostering long term cultural preservation through public education and awareness.
